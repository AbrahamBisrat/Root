The Strategy of Tit for Tat

In the iterated Prisoner's Dilemma, two opponents face a choice repeatedly: Cooperate or Defect. What is the optimal strategy when you don't know how many rounds you'll play?

In 1980, Robert Axelrod held a computer tournament to find out. The winner was the simplest program submitted: Tit for Tat.

The Algorithm

Tit for Tat follows just two simple rules. It requires no complex calculation, probability estimation, or memory beyond the previous turn.

01
Start Nice: Always Cooperate on the very first move.
02
Copycat: On every subsequent move, copy exactly what your opponent did in the previous move.

Why It Wins

Despite its simplicity, Tit for Tat dominated more complex strategies. Axelrod identified four properties that made it successful:

1. Nice

It never defects first. This prevents it from getting into unnecessary trouble with other nice strategies, allowing for "mutually assured cooperation".

2. Retaliatory

It strikes back immediately after being defected against. It cannot be exploited by "nasty" strategies effectively.

3. Forgiving

It doesn't hold a grudge. If the opponent goes back, it goes back. This stops endless feedback loops of retaliation.

4. Clear

Its behavior is easy to recognize. Opponents quickly learn that cooperation pays off and defection is punished.
